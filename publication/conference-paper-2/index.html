Prediction of Rock Fragmentation using a Novel Hybrid Evolutionary Algorithm and Extreme Gradient Boosting Machine

Duah Philip
November 2019
Cite Project Custom Link
Image credit: Duah Philip

Abstract

Rock fragmentation by blasting is regarded as one of the most substantial activities of the mine production cycle owing to its proportionate effect (cost) on subsequent operations such as loading, hauling and crushing. Being able to predict the fragmentation of a rock mass by blasting offers substantial advantage in providing muck pile with a desirable size distribution for crushing. Cognizant of this, various models have been developed over the years that predict the size distribution resulting from a blast design. Empirical and mechanistic techniques are the most popular fragmentation models. However, in the past few years there have been a consensus among researchers concerning inconsistencies associated with these models. For example, the Kuz-Ram model which is a popular empirical model is known to perform poorly in predicting the fines and upper limit block sizes resulting from blasting and also does not consider accurate timing or delay offered by modern electronic detonators. Although statistical and machine learning techniques have been heavily investigated in this domain, however, far too little attention has been paid to the influence of controllable and uncontrollable parameters on blasting and fragmentation modelling. Considering several sources of evidence in the current body of knowledge, it appears probable that a more accurate model is required. In this study, for the first time, we propose GA-XGBoost, a novel artificial intelligence algorithm by hybridizing Genetic Algorithm (GA) which is an evolutionary algorithm and Extreme Gradient Boosting Machine (XGBoost) for fragmentation modelling using data from two mines. The performance of GA-XGBoost is compared to Multivariate Adaptive Regression Splines (MARS) which is a non-parametric regression technique using statistical analysis. This study would provide an important opportunity to advance the understanding of the capability of hybrid artificial intelligence models and the impact of key blasting parameters in fragmentation modelling.
